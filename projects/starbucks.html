EasyOrder at Starbucks

Duration     Sept - Dec 2018
Teammate     David Howard / Shengxi Wu / Daier Yuan
My Role     UX & CX design / Interaction Design / Visual Design / User testing
Approaches     Evidence-based design / Task analysis / Journey map / Persona / Axure / Usability test




" Transforming in-store ordering experience at Starbucks "

Have you ever given up getting a cup of coffee when you saw a line without an end?
Have you ever been confused by mocha, latte, Frappuccino and many other technical terms?
Have you ever been given a weird name by baristas that keep you away from your orders?
Have you ever had some time feeling pressured to place an order through interpersonal communication? 

Despite the fact that Starbucks mobile app is gaining its popularity, people are using it mainly for pre-order or instant payment. After spending multiple afternoons at Starbucks for observations and interviewing a bunch of Starbucks visitors, we noticed that in-store congestion still resulted from inefficient on-spot ordering experience.


*Customer Journey Map made by Shengxi & Hanyu

/ Design Outcome /


Optimize Navigation Experience


Many users have complaint about the complex Starbucks terminology. We believe a more intuitive navigation can start from selecting beverage types. "I want..." statement makes the machine more human,  and graphical coffee menu helps users to distinguish "latte" from "Mocha".



Make Customization Easier


Customization menu as it is not a universal need. For people who want to customize their drinks but not sure how to create a better flavor, the customizing process could be very complicated as there are too many adds-on items. Therefore, recommended customization options are shaded in shallow brown in our final design.





Match Drinks To You With Fun!


If users don't want to use their own name, they can try the avatar generator. The avatar will be printed on the cup, and be displayed on the screen next to the counter when the drink is ready.



Express Tracks For Faster Purchase


Users can do self-checkout through the scanning function on the kiosks if they buy items like mugs, packaged sandwiches or bottle drinks, which will help to save baristas' time. In the meanwhile, Starbucks members can retrieve past orders so they don't need to repetitive work. This function is especially useful for those who always order same things at Starbucks.



   


/ What happened "in store"/


Before we finally decided to focus on in-store experience, we did a thorough research about Starbucks purchasing experience. To be specific, I was responsible for comparing the mobile app approach and traditional register experience. I interviewed both loyal Starbucks fans and occasional visitors to understand their thoughts and behavior. Together with my teammates, we did an affinity map to categorize the insights we collected. It turned out that most complaints were related to physical store experience, especially for customers who don't always use the mobile app to pre-order.



After narrowing down our scope to ordering process in the stores, we then did detailed task analysis to dissect users' action sequence based on non-intrusive observations and contextual inquiries.


Long waiting time during peak hours, occasional mistaken orders or names and unfamiliarity with the menu cause "congestion" in stores. If we want to solve the problems, the next question will be:Who are the users?




/ What customers need /


By summarizing all the users we've interviewd, we noticed that there were three groups of people who held different likes and dislikes for this world-famous coffee shop:



       (1) Coffee addict:loyal Starbucks members who go to the shop frequently and have rich knowledge in relevant products;
       (2) Occasional visitor:people who drop by Starbucks because of geographical convenience or friends accompany;
       (3) Leisure time killer: people who regard Starbucks as work/study environment;

To better understand users needs and draw design implications, I integrated personas and empathy maps to depict three typical characters:  





The second-round affinity mapping helped us to categorize users thoughts and needs. 

/ How we got to the solution /


Based on the conclusion we drew from previous research, we have decided that our design shall follow at least one of the following design goals:
- It shall enable more engaging and faster ordering process
- It shall help consumers, especially those unfamiliar with products, to understand the menu better
- It shall provide more convenient customization methods
- It shall help to match orders and consumers more efficiently




We were able to categorize over 30 concepts into trends and specific problems they were solving. After taking these concepts, recombining them, and comparing them against our own criteria, such as price, feasibility, and and ease of use, we brought ourselves down to three concepts we were prepared to make more refined sketches: a stand-alone kiosk, audio communication, and an innovative representation of a name. 

10 feedback sessions were organized to help us understand how users think about the concepts. Most of the participants were glad to see these new options for Starbucks purchasing, though each idea had its own pros and cons. It seems that among the three ideas, audio playback was the least popular one because users really care about their privacy and they thought hearing their own voices make things more uncomfortable.







/ Iterative Design Process /


We believed that a small series of in-store kiosks will be the most appropriate solution based on previous findings. We decided to combine the 1st and 2nd design idea to create a more detailed wireframe for the kiosk.  


*Low-fidelity prototype


We had 6 participants for our low-fidelity wireframe feedback sessions. All the feedbacks were documented in a consistent way following the planned questions, based on which I iterated the design. The overall feedback was rather positive, both Starbucks addicts and occasional visitors would like to try the kiosk in store. "I would do anything to avoid awkward communication with baristas." said one of our test participants.


*Refined work flow




Iteration focus I: How to navigate


Insight: "It takes time to order because I can't understand Starbucks menu. There are just too many products and too many technical terms. I feel stressed to ask baristas questions.".

Efficient ordering procedure comes from clear menu, from which unfamiliar customers can quickly extract the useful information and make decisions. I spent a long time on figuring out how to break traditional drink menu patterns.





Iteration focus II: How to meet needs of different-level customers


Insight: "I like customized drinks. Once I find my favorite, I'll order it every time until I'm sick of it."

Unlike Starbucks greenhands, customers who are familiar with coffee drinks and Starbucks need more shortcuts. How our design could benefit both novice visitors and old customers simultaneously became a challenge.





Iteration focus III: How to match customers and orders smartly


Insight: "Having baristas spell my name correctly is like winning a lottery. I need to guess if the drink belongs to me."

During peak hours, the shop is always crowded and noisy so it is hard for baristas to get customers names correctly. Mistakes occur more frequently if the customer has a foreign name. I strived to make the order matching approach more efficient while keeping it "human".



/ Evaluation /


We conducted 3 heuristic evaluation sessions with experts and 6 usability tests with different users to evaluate our design. To make the evaluations more contextualized, we built physical prototypes to simulate real kiosk using scenarios.


Heuristic evaluation was implemented before user tests, based on which we modified some small non-task related flaws on the interface.

The usability testing allowed us to observe users’ interaction with the kiosk, quantify the quality of our design through questionnaires, and get more insights into users' feelings about the use of the kiosk through follow-up questions and interviews.

By the end of evaluation sessions, we were excited to see that all participants expressed their interests in using our kiosk if Starbucks could put it into use.




/ Afterthoughts /


Designing digital interface for a kiosk is very different from mobile app or web design, especially when Starbucks already has a mature branding language system. I tried my best to make the interface and interactions as simple as possible, and to innovate without breaking existing Starbucks rules.

Also, this is the first project that I have experienced systematic usability testings along with the design process. Listening to users' voice and observing users behavior can really bring benefits to design iteration, and more importantly, downsize the gap between designers and users. I began to understand the science behind user-centered design through practice: every design decision shall be made based on solid reasons instead of abstract aesthetic preferences or imagination. I am glad that my team was able to get a large amount of information from our final hybrid prototype that turned into a substantial amount of qualitative and quantitative feedback. If we had chance to get a larger sample size of participants, we would be able to gain more quantitative data to support/doubt our design decisions.
